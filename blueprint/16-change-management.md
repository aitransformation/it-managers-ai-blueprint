# Section 16: Change Management

> **Journey Step:** Step 3 — Skill Up

---

## Why this section exists

Technology deployments that do not adequately address organisational change consistently underperform or fail. The IT Manager shall plan and execute a change management programme in parallel with every significant AI deployment — not after it has gone live.

---

## Communication standard

All staff affected by an AI deployment shall be informed **before** the change takes effect. Communication covers:
- What the tool will do and will not do
- How it affects their specific role
- What training they will receive
- How concerns are raised
- Who is accountable

**Communication is delivered in person** by the IT Manager or a senior sponsor. Not by email alone. Email is a follow-up, not the primary channel for meaningful change.

---

## Addressing AI anxiety directly

Job security concern related to AI shall be addressed directly, honestly, and early. Do not:
- Dismiss concerns as irrational
- Offer unsupportable reassurances ("this will never affect your job")
- Ignore the topic and hope it resolves itself

The honest position for most SME IT deployments is that AI is expanding operational capability, not reducing headcount — and that the skills required are changing. Concrete investment in those skills — visible training, involvement in deployments, recognition for engaged adoption — is more effective than reassurance alone.

If the IT Manager cannot honestly say that the deployment will not lead to headcount reduction, they should not pretend otherwise. Honesty builds trust. Discovered dishonesty destroys it.

**This is where the founding principle matters most.** *AI amplifies people. It does not replace them.* It needs to be true in practice, not just stated in a document.

---

## Internal champions

Identify individuals who engage readily with new technology early in the process. Brief them:
- Before the wider team — give them context and time to form their own view
- On the change management plan, not just the technology
- With access to test environments — let them develop genuine familiarity

Champions are the most effective channel for peer-level adoption support. They will surface practical issues that formal feedback mechanisms miss. Formally recognise their contribution — visibility matters.

---

## Feedback loops

Establish a monthly feedback session — 30 minutes, open to all affected staff. This is not a status update meeting. It is a session where the IT Manager listens to what is and is not working, and where concerns are addressed.

The monthly feedback session is not optional after deployment. If no one raises issues, that is useful information too.

---

## Implementation checklist

- [ ] Identify all affected staff groups for each planned deployment — plan communications for each
- [ ] Hold a team briefing before each deployment — allow time for genuine questions
- [ ] Identify two internal champions — involve in testing before wider rollout
- [ ] Deliver appropriate training tier to all affected staff before deployment (see Section 14)
- [ ] Establish a monthly feedback session — 30 minutes, open to all
- [ ] Address AI anxiety directly in the team briefing — specific, honest, with evidence of skills investment
