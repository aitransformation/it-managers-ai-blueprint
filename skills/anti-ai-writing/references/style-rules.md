# Style rules

## Core rewrite heuristics

### Prefer direct language
- “is” beats “serves as”
- “helps” beats “facilitates”
- “use” beats “leverage”
- “because” beats “due to the fact that”

### Prefer specifics over fog
If the draft says something is important, useful, effective, or transformative, ask: how exactly?

### Let sentences breathe
Mix lengths naturally.
Short sentences are fine.
Longer ones are fine too, when they earn their space.

### Keep opinions where they help
Writing sounds more human when it has a view, not just posture.

### Don’t fake humanity
Avoid defaulting to:
- typos
- quirky asides
- fake self-interruptions
- slang that doesn’t fit the audience

## Safe editing moves

- delete the first sentence if it only clears its throat
- delete the last sentence if it only waves goodbye
- collapse two weak sentences into one strong one
- replace abstract nouns with concrete examples
- cut one adjective before adding another
- if a source is unnamed, either name it or remove the appeal to authority

## Tone guardrails

### For professional copy
- calm
- specific
- unbloated
- confident without swagger

### For thought leadership
- say something real
- avoid guru language
- prefer observation over grandstanding

### For educational writing
- be clear before being elegant
- examples beat abstractions
- avoid over-framing simple ideas

## Final litmus test

A strong line usually sounds like somebody means it.
A weak line usually sounds like it was generated to occupy the shape of meaning.
